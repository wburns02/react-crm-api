Why You've Been Told to Have Your System Pumped Out
Your System Has 4 Compartments All aerobic septic systems are made up of four main compartments. Wastewater flows through each one in order. Understanding how they work helps you understand why pump-outs are sometimes necessary.
1 Trash Tank Collects heavy solids and non-degradable items before they enter the treatment process
→
2 Aerobic Treatment Tank Bacteria break down organic waste using oxygen from the aerator/blower
→
3 Settling Tank Remaining solids settle to the bottom; clarified water rises to the top
→
4 Pump Tank Treated water is pumped out to spray heads or drip field for disposal
The Problem: Hydraulic Overload

When we inspect your system and find solids accumulating in the pump tank — with very little or no solids in the aerobic treatment tank — it tells us you have, or have had, a hydraulic overload.

This means too much water entered the system too fast, pushing solids through the treatment process before the bacteria had time to break them down. Those solids end up in the pump tank where they don't belong.

Common Causes of Hydraulic Overload
🚽 Running toilets — even a slow leak sends hundreds of gallons per day into the system
❄️ Running water during a freeze — dripping faucets can overwhelm the system overnight
👚 Laundry days — multiple back-to-back loads (one in the morning and one in the evening is what we recommend)
🛁 Emptying large bathtubs quickly — put a wash cloth on the drain to slow the water leaving the tub
⚗️ Water softener malfunctions — a stuck valve can dump the entire brine tank into the system
🌧️ Unsealed tanks and heavy rains — groundwater and rainwater infiltrate through cracked lids or unsealed risers
What Happens If You Don't Pump It Out
1
Your pump has a suction inlet at 8 inches from the bottom of the tank
2
At 6 to 7 inches, accumulated solids are being suctioned directly through the pump
3
Solids clog the spray heads, which begin to back up
4
Once spray heads clog, the pump works against back pressure, causing damage to the plastic impellers
5
Continued operation under back pressure generates heat, further damaging the pump
6
Result: you'll need both the pump tank and trash tank pumped out completely
The Bottom Line A pump-out now prevents costly pump and spray head repairs later. When we recommend a pump-out, it's because we've seen the warning signs during your inspection. Addressing it early saves you money and keeps your system running properly.
How to Prevent Hydraulic Overload
  • Fix running toilets immediately — this is the #1 cause
  • Spread laundry out — one load in the morning, one in the evening
  • Slow-drain bathtubs with a washcloth over the drain
  • During freezes, keep dripping faucets to a bare minimum
  • Have your water softener inspected annually
  • Make sure tank lids and risers are properly sealed to keep rainwater out
  • Keep your quarterly maintenance contract current — we catch these problems early
