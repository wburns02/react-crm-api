Real Estate Agent Conversation Guide
How to introduce MAC Septic to realtors and earn the inspection referral
What This Guide Is For Real estate agents are a high-value referral channel. Every home with a septic system that changes hands needs a pre-sale inspection — and the agent picks who does it. This guide gives Dannia a natural, conversational way to introduce MAC Septic, explain the inspection process, and become the agent's go-to provider. The goal is 3–6 real estate inspections per week per agent relationship.
Part 1 — Opening the Conversation

Real estate agents are busy and get pitched constantly. Lead with curiosity, not a sales pitch. The first goal is to find out if they currently work with a septic inspector and whether they're happy with them.

The Opener:
Hi, this is Dannia with MAC Septic Services. I'm not selling you anything — I'm just calling to introduce us. Do you have a minute? We do real estate septic inspections in the Nashville area, and I wanted to see who you usually work with for those when one of your listings has a septic system.
If They Say They Already Have Someone:
That's great. Most agents already have someone. I just like to introduce us as a backup — sometimes inspectors get booked out a week or two when you've got a tight closing. Can I send you our info so you've got us on file if you ever need a quick turnaround?
If They Say They Don't Have a Regular Inspector:
Perfect, then I'd love to tell you a little about us. Two minutes — just want to make sure when you do have a listing with a septic system, you know who to call.
The Mindset Don't ask for the business on the first call. Ask to be the backup. The vast majority of agents already have an inspector — but those relationships break down constantly (slow scheduling, sloppy reports, missed appointments). When the regular guy lets them down, they remember whoever they liked the most from their last intro call.

For you, that translates to one thing: when I send a tech to your listing, your seller and your buyer both have a clean, professional experience. No surprise charges, no missed appointments, no half-finished reports. That's what I want you to associate with us.

Part 4 — What a Real Estate Inspection Looks Like

Most agents don't actually know what's inside a septic inspection. Walk them through it so they sound smart to their buyer.

The Walk-Through:
Every real estate inspection includes a pump-out — that's mandatory because you can't actually inspect the inside of the tank with sewage in it. Once it's pumped, the tech goes in and checks four things: the walls and floors of the tank for cracks, the ceiling of the tank for root intrusion (which is huge because if you catch it early you can save the system), the pump itself, and the distribution to the field. Then we write up a detailed inspection report — not just a pass/fail. We tell you exactly what condition the system is in, point out anything we found, and if there's an issue we tell you what it'll take to fix it. Your client and the lender both get a clear picture of what they're walking into.
Why the Pump-Out Actually Matters (the part most people get wrong):
Here's something most homeowners and even some agents don't realize — the tank is supposed to have water and effluent in it. That's not the problem. The problem is the scum layer on top and the solids on the bottom. Those are what cause the failures. When the solids build up, they push out into the pump chamber and the drain field, which is what kills the pump and clogs the field. A pump replacement runs $3,000 to $5,000. A field replacement runs $5,000 to $7,000. So the pump-out isn't just “cleaning the tank” — it's removing the exact stuff that destroys the most expensive parts of the system.
The 30-Second Education This is the single best thing Dannia can teach an agent. Most agents have never been told this. Once they understand it, they explain it to every client themselves — and you've turned them into a referral engine. They'll say “you have to get it pumped because the scum and solids will destroy your pump and field — that's a $3K–$5K pump or a $5K–$7K field, all to avoid an $825 inspection.” That's MAC's pitch coming out of the agent's mouth.
The Detailed Report Pitch:
A lot of inspectors hand you a one-line letter that says “system functional.” That doesn't help anybody. Our reports include direct feedback on the actual state of the system, photos when relevant, and clear recommendations for any repairs we found — with rough costs so your client isn't blindsided. If the system is great, we say so. If something needs attention, you find out before closing, not after.
Why It's Worth It (For the Buyer):
The whole point is your buyer's peace of mind. They walk into that house knowing the septic system is good for the next 3 to 5 years before they need to think about it again. That's a huge stress reducer at closing.

What Gets Inspected:

  • Tank walls and floors — checking for cracks that lead to leaks or groundwater intrusion
  • Tank ceiling — checking for root intrusion (cheap to fix early, expensive to fix late)
  • Pump and floats — making sure the pump runs and the floats trigger correctly
  • Drain field / distribution — verifying effluent is being distributed evenly to the field
  • Detailed inspection report — full written feedback on system condition, photos, and resolution recommendations with rough costs if anything needs attention
  • Delivered to the payer — agent, buyer, or seller, whoever paid the invoice

Part 6 — The Two Disclaimers (Less Than 5% of Jobs)

Be upfront about the rare extra-fee situations. Telling the agent about them BEFORE the job builds massive trust. Surprising them WITH the bill destroys the relationship.

1. Lid Access — Buried Tank
If the septic tank lid is buried under landscaping, dirt, concrete, or pavers, the tech has to dig it out before the pump-out can happen. We can usually find it with a probe in a few minutes — but if there's serious digging involved, that's billed by the hour.
Fee: $150/hour for digging
2. Cement Solids — Heavily Compacted Sludge
When a tank hasn't been pumped in 10+ years, or if the homeowner has been using additives like Rid-X, the solids at the bottom can get as hard as cement. It just takes longer to break up and remove. Same hourly rate.
Fee: $150/hour for extended pump time
How to Pitch the Disclaimers:
There are two situations where a job runs over the flat rate, and they hit less than 5% of the homes we touch. One is if the tank lid is buried and we have to dig — that's $150 an hour for the digging. The other is if the sludge has gotten really hard, which happens when a tank hasn't been pumped in a long time. Same rate. The reason I'm telling you upfront, before we ever schedule, is so you're not surprised. We can't stop a job mid-dig and wait hours for a callback — the tech has to keep moving — but you'll already know these situations are possible because we're talking about them right now. Anything outside the standard $825 gets itemized clearly on the invoice.
Why This Builds Trust Most septic companies don't mention edge cases until the bill arrives. Agents have been burned by this. Telling them upfront — with a number, in plain English — flips the dynamic. The agent already knew the disclaimers were possible before they ever booked the job, so when one comes up the invoice isn't a fight, it's an expectation. You become the inspector they trust precisely because you led with the uncomfortable part.

The Process Pitch:
The flow is really simple. You call us, or your client calls us — whoever's easier. We get on the schedule, usually within 3 to 5 business days. Tech goes out, pumps the tank, inspects everything, comes back to the office. We write up the inspection letter, send the invoice to whoever's paying, and as soon as that's paid, the letter goes to the same person. That's it. You don't have to chase anybody.
One Important Detail The inspection letter goes to the person who paid for the inspection. So if the seller pays, it goes to the seller. If the buyer pays, it goes to the buyer. If the agent pays and bills it back, it goes to the agent. Be explicit about this on the call so there's no confusion at closing.
Part 8 — Closing the Conversation

Don't ask for a commitment. Ask to send a one-pager and to follow up in a few weeks. Building a referral relationship takes 3–5 touches, not one.

The Soft Close:
I really appreciate you taking a couple minutes. Can I send you a one-page summary of what we just talked about? That way it's in your inbox when you need it. And do you mind if I check back in a few weeks just to see if anything's come up where I can help?
If They Have a Listing With a Septic System Right Now:
Actually — do you have anything on the market right now with a septic system? I'd love to put a quote together for you while we're talking, just so you know what to tell your seller. It's no obligation. I can have it in your inbox in 10 minutes.
Always Ask For:
  • Their preferred email for the one-pager and quotes
  • Their cell phone for fast turnaround on hot listings
  • Permission to follow up in 2–4 weeks
  • If they say they're happy with their current inspector: ask who they use — helps us know who we're up against in this market
The Golden Rules of Realtor Calls (1) Don't pitch — introduce. (2) Ask to be the backup, not the primary. (3) Lead with the disclaimers, not the bill. (4) Promise an inspection letter in their inbox before they hang up if they have an active listing. (5) Always confirm a follow-up window before ending the call — the relationship is built over months, not minutes.
