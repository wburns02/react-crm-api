Does Texas Law Require a Maintenance Contract?
Yes. If you have an aerobic septic system, Texas state law requires you to have an active maintenance contract with a TCEQ-licensed provider.
30 Texas Administrative Code, Chapter 285, Subchapter H (§285.90–285.91) • Texas Health & Safety Code, Chapter 366
Who Does This Apply To?
Contract REQUIRED by Law • All aerobic treatment systems
• Spray distribution systems
• Drip irrigation systems
• Surface application systems
• Non-standard treatment systems
Applies to ALL systems — old and new. There is no grandfathering.
Contract NOT Required • Conventional septic (gravity-fed)
• Standard leach field / drain field

Conventional systems should still be pumped every 3–5 years, but there is no legal requirement for a maintenance contract.
Common Questions
Q: My system was installed 20 years ago. Does this still apply to me?
Yes. The maintenance contract requirement applies to every aerobic system in Texas, regardless of when it was installed. There is no grandfathering or exemption for older systems.
Q: Can I maintain the system myself instead of hiring a company?
TCEQ rules allow homeowner self-maintenance on single-family residences after the first 2 years. However, most Central Texas counties — including Travis, Hays, and Bexar — have adopted stricter local rules that require a licensed provider regardless. Check with your county.
Q: What does a maintenance visit include?
A licensed provider inspects all four compartments, tests disinfection (chlorine/UV), checks the aerator and pumps, tests alarms, inspects spray heads or drip lines, measures sludge levels, and files a report with the county. Visits are typically quarterly.
Q: How much does a maintenance contract cost?
Most providers in Central Texas charge $250–$500 per year for quarterly service. That's less than $1.50/day to protect a system that costs $15,000–$30,000+ to replace.
What Happens If You Don't Have a Contract?
Consequence Details
Notice of Violation The county sends a written notice. You typically have 30 days to get a contract in place.
Daily Fines Counties can impose fines of $50–$200 per day of non-compliance.
Home Sale Complications Most counties now require proof of an active contract and a passing inspection at closing. A lapsed contract can delay or block your home sale.
TCEQ Penalties For serious violations, TCEQ can impose penalties up to $10,000 per day under Texas Water Code §7.102.
System Failure Without regular maintenance, aerobic systems fail. A failed system means raw sewage on your property, health hazards, and a $15,000–$30,000+ replacement bill.

Where Exactly Does the Law Say This?
What This Means in Plain English Every aerobic system in Texas must have a maintenance contract (§285.33(c)(3)). For the first 2 years, it must be with a licensed provider (§285.7(c)). After 2 years, single-family homeowners can technically self-maintain (§285.7(d)(4)) — but most Central Texas counties have adopted stricter local rules that require a licensed provider regardless. If your contract lapses, you have 30 days to get a new one (§285.7(d)).
1 §285.7(a) — The Gateway
“Maintenance contract requirements for all on-site sewage facilities (OSSFs) are identified in §285.91(12) of this title. The permit holder shall ensure that the OSSF is properly operated and maintained in accordance with this chapter.”
2 §285.33(c)(3) — The Requirement for Aerobic Systems
“Ongoing maintenance contracts are required for all proprietary treatment systems except those systems maintained by homeowners under the provisions of §285.7(d)(4).”
“Proprietary treatment systems” = aerobic units (Norweco, Fuji Clean, Clearstream, Jet, etc.). Every aerobic system sold in Texas is a proprietary treatment system.
3 §285.7(d) — Contract Is Mandatory; Self-Maintenance Is the Exception
“If a maintenance contract is discontinued or terminated, the owner shall contract with another maintenance provider and provide the permitting authority with a copy of the new signed maintenance contract no later than 30 days after termination, unless the owner meets the requirements of paragraph (4).”
4 §285.7(d)(4) — The Self-Maintenance Exception (and Its Limits)
“At the end of the initial two-year service policy, the owner of an OSSF for a single family residence shall either maintain the system personally or obtain a new maintenance contract.”
“An owner may not maintain an OSSF under the provisions of this section for commercial, speculative residential, or multifamily property.”
5 §285.7(c) — First 2 Years: No Self-Maintenance Allowed
“The initial two-year service policy shall be effective for two years from the date the OSSF is first used… the individual fulfilling the service policy shall be a maintenance provider or a maintenance technician working under the supervision of a maintenance provider.”
