County-by-County Septic Rules for Central Texas
Why County Rules Matter TCEQ sets the minimum standards for aerobic septic systems statewide (30 TAC Chapter 285). But your county acts as the local enforcer and can — and often does — adopt stricter rules. That means your obligations depend on where you live. Here's what each county requires beyond the state baseline.

Travis County — Transportation & Natural Resources (TNR) 512-854-9383
Self-maintenance: PROHIBITED • Reporting: EVERY 4 MONTHS • Governed by: Travis County Code Chapter 448
  • Licensed provider required — Travis County effectively prohibits homeowner self-maintenance on aerobic systems. You must have a contract with a licensed maintenance provider at all times.
  • Reporting every 4 months — Providers must submit maintenance reports to the county at least once every 4 months. Reports submitted via the county's online portal.
  • Recorded affidavit — An "Affidavit to the Public for OSSF Requiring Maintenance" is filed with the county, notifying future property owners of the maintenance obligation.
  • LCRA lake zone — Properties within 2,000 feet of Lake Travis fall under LCRA jurisdiction (not Travis County). LCRA has its own separate OSSF rules and permitting. Contact: 512-578-3216.
  • Enforcement — Daily inspection fees accrue until violations are corrected. Referral to County Attorney for civil penalties and/or criminal charges.
Hays County — Development Services 512-393-2150
Self-maintenance: PROHIBITED • Reporting: EVERY 4 MONTHS • Edwards Aquifer Protection Plan may be required
  • Licensed provider required — Hays County requires ongoing maintenance with a licensed provider for all aerobic/advanced treatment systems. The self-maintenance exception does not apply here.
  • Reporting every 4 months — Providers submit maintenance reports at least once every 4 months with a specific inspection form format.
  • Edwards Aquifer Recharge Zone — Properties on the recharge or contributing zone must provide proof of an Edwards Aquifer Protection Plan (30 TAC Chapter 213 requirements layered on top of Chapter 285).
  • All lot sizes — A permit is required for all OSSF regardless of lot or acreage size.
  • Enforcement — Hays County has pursued fines against homeowners with failed or unmaintained systems. Complaints: 512-393-2150, option 3.
Bexar County — Environmental Services 210-335-6700
Self-maintenance: EFFECTIVELY PROHIBITED • Reporting: EVERY 4 MONTHS • SARA-certified providers
  • Continuous service contract required — Homeowners must maintain a service contract with a licensed provider and submit it to Bexar County. The language is mandatory, not optional.
  • Reporting every 4 months — Providers must submit maintenance reports to Bexar County Post Construction at least once every 4 months.
  • SARA certification — Maintenance provider certification must be approved by the San Antonio River Authority (SARA).
  • Lapse penalty — If maintenance lapses for over 1 year or quarterly reports are not received, a violation letter is sent and a renewal permit with engineer certification is required to bring the site back into compliance.
  • Conventional systems — Must renew every 5 years via tank pumping receipt + $30 fee.
  • Seven violation categories — Including expired permits, expired contracts, illegal discharge (most serious), inadequate capacity, unpermitted systems.
Comal County — Environmental Health 830-608-2090
Self-maintenance: ALLOWED (single-family) • Reporting: EVERY 4 MONTHS • Two-strike rule
  • Homeowner self-maintenance allowed — Single-family dwelling owners may self-maintain with no training requirement, no mandatory reports, and no notification to the county. Commercial properties cannot self-maintain.
  • Reporting every 4 months — Licensed providers submit testing reports to the county at least once every 4 months, with annual provider registration required.
  • Two-strike rule — If you receive two violations within 3 years, you lose the self-maintenance privilege and must enter a mandatory licensed maintenance contract within 10 days.
  • 10-day cure period — After a violation notice, you have 10 days to correct it or enter a licensed contract.
  • Property transfer — OSSF permit must be transferred to the buyer. $150 reinspection fee if system fails or is not ready.
Guadalupe County — Environmental Health 830-303-8858
Self-maintenance: NOT PROHIBITED • Reporting: EVERY 4 MONTHS (TCEQ baseline) • 1-acre minimum lot size
  • Follows TCEQ baseline — Maintenance frequency matches the state standard (every 4 months). No published prohibition on homeowner self-maintenance.
  • 1-acre minimum lot size — New OSSF installations require a minimum of 1 acre (exceeds TCEQ's 0.5-acre minimum). Lots subdivided before January 1, 1998 may be grandfathered.
  • License to Operate — A county-issued License to Operate is required before using any OSSF.
  • Connecting to existing systems — A permit is required even to connect into an existing OSSF (not just new installations).
  • 30-day decision — County must approve or deny permit applications within 30 days of receipt.
The Bottom Line for Homeowners If you live in Travis, Hays, or Bexar County, you cannot legally self-maintain your aerobic system — you need a licensed provider, period. In Comal County, you can self-maintain but two violations and you lose that right. In Guadalupe County, requirements track the state baseline. Regardless of county, a professional maintenance contract is the safest way to stay compliant, protect your system, and avoid fines.
